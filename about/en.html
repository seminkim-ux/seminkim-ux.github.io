Philosophy - Core ideas behind my work.

Although I moved from editorial design to UX, I believe the essence of both is communication. It's communication that shifted from print media to digital devices.

For effective communication, the message must be clear, and "readability" is the most important aspect of visual presentation.
More fundamentally, I need to judge whether content is being conveyed in a way that users can easily understand, and whether we're not assuming users know what service creators know. That is the foundation of my work.

At a higher level, I was influenced by "Jan Tschichold's Typography" which I learned during my visual design major. At a time when many designers were showing off their aesthetic sense with decorative typography, he focused on the content that needed to be communicated through typography and advocated for the most effective delivery of content.
While this seems obvious, it's sometimes overlooked.

If Jan Tschichold provided a foundational influence, I continue to draw inspiration from the design studio where I first started my career and other domestic and international design studios. Pentagram and Ideo are typical examples.

Experience - Proof of deep expertise and flexible responsiveness.

Over 10+ years of researching various services and working across entire domains to realize them has given me definite strength.

1. I understand various UX, UI components and their effectiveness.

Thanks to my team leader who had us systematically study and organize Apple's Human Interface Guide and Google's Material Design Guide every year, I built structured knowledge. I've internalized this knowledge through extensive practical application.

2. I can extend planning beyond the screen.

Screen planning begins with the big picture of service planning, but sometimes various needs and possibilities emerge from concrete details. Service directions and policies that emerge during the concretization process strongly represent the needs of services and users. I've been realizing these possibilities.

3. Above all, it's exciting to discover and see more content.

I enjoy football, but I don't fully understand discussions about formations or tactics. In Netflix's hit show "The Chef" when chefs discover and admire each other's cooking techniques saying "Ah, so that's how you did it," I don't fully understand that either.

However, when it comes to the services and screens I work on, I can see what needs to be done, what the best approach is, and how to make something stand out, which I find enjoyable. It's exciting to reach a level where I can discuss the efforts others have made in their work and identify areas for improvement.

This website was created using Claude.

Claude.ai excels at code writing, but still lacks the ability to easily create a desired site. Therefore, building this site took quite some time, and I had to continuously fix errors in developer mode.

The process involved asking how to do things, requesting code creation, and testing for errors. Sometimes there were interaction errors that couldn't be resolved, so I requested simplifying the content first and then applying it again. I felt that there are still many areas where human intervention is necessary, which was a relief.
